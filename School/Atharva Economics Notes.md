**Economics Defined**: Economics is the study of how agents (individuals, families, firms, organizations, governments) choose to allocate scarce resources and how those choices affect society.

  
**Economics as a Lens: We use four key perspectives  
**

- Tradeoffs (benefits and costs)
- Choices (preferences and rationality)
- Markets (for better and/or worse)
- Empiricism (data-driven analysis)

**The Three Fundamental Principles:  
**1. Optimization:  

- Economic agents try to pick the best feasible option given their limited information and resources
- Optimization at the Margin: An optimal choice means moving toward it makes you better off, moving away makes you worse off

2. Equilibrium  

- The special situation where everyone is simultaneously optimizing
- No one believes they would benefit by changing their behavior, given others' choices

3. Empiricism  

- Using real-world data to test economic models and theories
- Essential for understanding how economics actually works in practice

  
**Micro vs. Macro Economics:**  

- Microeconomics: Studies how individuals, households, firms, and governments make choices and how these affect prices, resource allocation, and other agents' well-being.
- Macroeconomics: Studies the economy as a whole - looking at aggregate patterns and national/global economic trends.

  
**Positive vs. Normative Economics:**  

- Positive Economics: Describes what people actually do. Can be tested and proven correct or incorrect.

Example: "An increase in income tax rates reduces disposable income."  

- Normative Economics: Describes what people ought to do. Inherently subjective and value-based.

Example: "The government should reduce taxes to improve the economy."

  
**Supply and Demand Fundamentals**

- Demand: The amount buyers are willing to purchase at different prices
- Willingness to Pay (WTP): Highest price a buyer will pay for an extra unit
- Demand Curve: Plots quantity demanded at different prices
- Supply: The amount sellers are willing to sell at different prices
- Willingness to Accept (WTA): Lowest price a seller will accept for an extra unit
- Supply Curve: Plots quantity supplied at different prices

  
**Key Concepts:**  

- Equilibrium: Where supply and demand curves intersect
- Price Ceiling: Maximum legal price (can cause shortages)
- Price Floor: Minimum legal price (can cause surpluses)
- Opportunity Cost: What you give up to use a resource

  
**The Invisible Hand  
**Adam Smith's concept explaining how individual self-interest can lead to societal benefits through:  

- Self-interest (optimization)
- Competition (constraints on behavior)
- Trust (enables efficient commerce)
- Rules/Institutions (property rights, infrastructure)

  
**Time Value of Money  
**Key Principle: Money today is more valuable than money tomorrow because it can be invested and grow.  
Essential Formulas:  

- Future Value: FV = PV(1+r)^n
- Present Value: PV = FV/(1+r)^n
- Where: PV = Present Value, FV = Future Value, r = interest rate, n = number of periods

- Rule of 72: Divide 72 by the interest rate to estimate how long it takes to double an investment.
- Fisher Equation: Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate - Inflation
**Comparative Advantage**: The ability of one economic agent to produce at lower opportunity cost than another. This is the key concept for understanding who should specialize in what.

**Production Possibility Curve (PPC)**: A graphical representation showing the maximum possible output combinations of two goods an economy can produce with its available resources.

**Key Trade Concepts**:

- **Absolute Advantage**: Being better at producing something than others
- **Comparative Advantage**: Having a lower opportunity cost in producing something
- **Specialization**: Focusing on what you do best (lowest opportunity cost)
- **Gains from Trade**: Countries can consume beyond their individual PPCs through specialization and trade

### Social Benefits of Trade:

1. Comparative advantage and specialization increase overall social welfare
2. Learning by doing generates additional expertise
3. Technology transfer across countries
4. Increased cultural exchange

### Social Costs of Trade:

1. National security concerns from over-reliance on other countries
2. Cultural protection and value preservation
3. Environmental concerns from varied standards
4. Infant industry protection needs
5. Negative effects on some workers

### Trade Policy Tools:

- **Trade Agreements**: Bilateral (two countries), multilateral/regional (several countries), or global (WTO)
- **Trade Barriers**: Tariffs (taxes on imports), quotas (quantity limits), subsidies (government support), import bans, and standards

## **Game Theory**: A mathematical framework for analyzing strategic decision-making where outcomes depend on choices made by multiple players.

**Key Elements**:

- **Players**: The decision-makers
- **Strategies**: Actions each player can take
- **Payoffs**: Rewards or penalties based on strategy combinations

**Types of Games**:

- **Simultaneous-move games**: All players decide at once (like rock-paper-scissors)
- **Sequential-move games**: Players take turns (like tic-tac-toe)

**Nash Equilibrium**: A situation where no player wants to change their strategy given what others are doing - everyone is making their best response.

### Games We Played:

- **P-Beauty Contest**: Choosing numbers to be closest to 2/3 of the average (Nash equilibrium = 0)
- **Prisoner's Dilemma**: Classic cooperation vs. betrayal scenario showing how individual rationality can lead to collectively worse outcomes

## Behavioral Economics and Psychology

- **Traditional Model**: "Homo economicus" - perfectly rational, selfish, and self-controlled 
- **Reality**: People are smart and respond to incentives, but often fall short of perfect rationality

### Key Behavioral Concepts:

**Present Bias**: People value immediate rewards more than future ones

- Example: Choosing chocolate now vs. waiting for two chocolates later
- Leads to procrastination and poor long-term planning

**Choice Architecture and Nudges**:

- **Opt-in vs. Opt-out**: Default options dramatically affect participation
- **Commitment devices**: Tools that help people stick to good intentions
- **Active choice**: Forcing decisions increases engagement

**When Psychology Matters Most**:

- When people are inexperienced
- In face-to-face negotiations
- When incentives are misaligned
- With immediate rewards and strong temptations
- When it's easy to procrastinate

## Financial Markets and Instruments

**Primary Markets**: Where securities are first issued (IPOs, bond issuances) **Secondary Markets**: Where existing securities trade (stock exchanges, bond markets)

### The Three Financial Statements:

#### 1. Income Statement

Shows how a company turns revenue into net income over a period.

**Key Formula**: Net Sales - COGS = Gross Profit → Operating Income (EBIT) → Net Income

**Important Ratios**:

- **Gross Profit Margin**: (Gross Profit / Revenue) × 100%
- **Operating Margin**: (Operating Income / Revenue) × 100%
- **Net Profit Margin**: (Net Income / Revenue) × 100%

#### 2. Balance Sheet

Shows financial position at a specific point in time.

**Fundamental Equation**: Assets = Liabilities + Equity

**Key Components**:

- **Current Assets**: Cash, accounts receivable, inventory
- **Non-Current Assets**: Property, plant & equipment, intangible assets
- **Current Liabilities**: Accounts payable, short-term debt
- **Non-Current Liabilities**: Long-term debt
- **Equity**: Common stock, retained earnings
**Perfect Competition Recap:**  

- Many buyers and sellers, no market power
- Firms are price takers
- Economic profits driven to zero in long run
- Maximizes total economic surplus

**Monopoly Fundamentals:**  

- Single seller with no close substitutes
- Firm becomes a price maker with market power
- Barriers to entry include patents, resource control, network effects, and economies of scale
- Profit maximization: Set quantity where MR = MC, then use demand curve to find price

**Price Discrimination:**  

- First-degree: Charge each buyer their maximum willingness to pay
- Second-degree: Bulk discounts and quantity-based pricing
- Third-degree: Different prices for different groups (student/senior discounts)

**Government Responses to Monopolies:**  

- Public provision of goods
- Price regulation
- Antitrust enforcement and patent reform

**1. Monetary Policy  
Federal Reserve Fundamentals:**  

- The Fed is an independent central bank created in 1913
- Dual Mandate: Price stability and maximum employment
- Controls short-term interest rates through the federal funds rate
- Uses Interest on Reserves (IOR) to influence bank lending rates

**Policy Tools:**  

- Open Market Operations: Buying/selling Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities
- Expansionary Policy: Lower rates → higher bond prices → increased investment and consumption
- Contractionary Policy: Higher rates → lower bond prices → reduced economic activity
- Zero Lower Bound Tools: Forward guidance and Quantitative Easing (QE) when rates hit zero

**Economic Impact:**  

- Lower rates increase consumption (C) and investment (I)
- Shifts aggregate demand right: AD = C + I + G + (X - M)
- Effects have "long and variable lags" - full impact may take a year

**2. Fiscal Policy  
Discretionary Fiscal Policy:**  

- Intentional changes in government spending (G) or taxes (T)
- Expansionary: Increase spending or cut taxes during recessions
- Contractionary: Decrease spending or raise taxes during booms

**Fiscal Multipliers:**  

- Measure output change per dollar of fiscal stimulus
- Unemployment benefits: ~1.2x multiplier
- Stimulus checks: ~0.8x multiplier
- Business tax cuts: ~0.1x multiplier
- Higher multipliers when recipients have high marginal propensity to consume

**Automatic Stabilizers:**  

- Built-in features that respond to economic changes without new legislation
- Examples: Progressive taxes, unemployment insurance, income-tested benefits
- Provided immediate COVID-19 relief before CARES Act passed

**3. Exchange Rate Policy  
Key Economic Identities:**  

- Net Exports (NX) = Exports - Imports
- National Saving (S) = Y - C - G
- NX = S - I (trade balance reflects saving-investment gap)

**Understanding Trade Deficits:**  

- Not necessarily harmful - often signal strong domestic economy
- Don't directly reduce GDP or jobs
- Can be "good" (reflecting strong investment) or "bad" (reflecting unsustainable borrowing)

**International Economics Case Studies  
We examined different countries' trade situations:**  

- Argentina: Bad deficit due to weak policies and capital flight
- Norway: "Good" deficit from oil investment, backed by sovereign wealth
- Germany: Large surplus from high saving and investment abroad
- China: Persistent surplus from high forced saving and lower domestic consumption
